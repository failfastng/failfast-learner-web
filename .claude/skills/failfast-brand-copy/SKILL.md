# FailFast Brand Copy Skill

This skill encodes everything learned from building the FailFast Copy Deck v9 across
multiple editorial sessions. It exists so any agent or model writing FailFast copy
produces output that is consistent, honest, and on-brand — whether it is a two-word
button label or a full marketing email.

---

## 1. The Platform in One Paragraph

FailFast is a skill-based knowledge competition platform built around a single idea:
failing is the first step to knowing. The scoring system rewards every attempt, including
wrong answers. Users practice in FailFast Mine, build a subject-specific tier ranking, and
compete in group challenges for real naira. FailFast EDU brings the same fail-forward
mechanics to schools and tutorial centers without any real-money features. FailFast Arena
is the competitive B2C side. Both products run on the same dual-point scoring system:
Grit Points (earned for effort and persistence) and Success Points (earned only for correct
answers).

## 3. Voice and Tone

**The FailFast voice sounds like a Nigerian who has been through exam season.** Direct,
honest, specific about numbers. If the product does something, say exactly what it does.
If it does not do something yet, say that too.

**Three rules every piece of copy must follow:**

1. Make one clear point. Copy that tries to make three points makes none.
2. End with what the user gets, not what the platform does. "You earn more points" beats
   "The platform awards more points."
3. Cut any sentence whose only job is to say how great the product is. The scoring table,
   pricing table, and tier system are the evidence. Let them do the work.

**Tone by context:**

| Context | Tone |
|---------|------|
| Hero headlines | Short, punchy, verb-forward. Three phrases max. |
| Body copy | Direct, factual, first-person where possible ("you earn", "your rank") |
| How It Works steps | Process-focused. One step, one action, one outcome. |
| Pricing copy | Specific numbers only. No rounding or hedging. |
| Beta/regulatory notices | Honest and plain. No spin. State the limitation then explain why. |
| Error or system messages | Non-accusatory. State what happened, what to do next. |
| Marketing emails | Start with the user's action or situation, not the platform's feature. |
| Push notifications | One sentence. Outcome-first. No filler. |

## 5. Numbers and Specifics

Always use the real number. Never approximate where a specific figure is known.

**Commissions:**
- Group staking: 10% flat
- Sponsored competitions (funded): 15% flat
- Challenger-hosted competitions (funded): 5% flat
- Instructor/Teacher wallet withdrawal: 1%
- Challenger wallet withdrawal: 3%
- NGO referral commission: 12% of B2B school revenue

**Tiers and stakes (Arena):**
- Rookie: no staking, gift cards only (up to 5,000 naira wallet)
- Skilled: 500–5,000 naira per challenge
- Expert: 5,001–15,000 naira per challenge
- Master: 15,001–35,000 naira per challenge
- Grand Master: 35,001–70,000 naira per challenge

**EDU pricing (per additional account, per term):**
- Student: 1,000 naira
- Teacher: 2,000 naira
- Admin: 3,000 naira
- Free allocation: 6 students, 2 teachers, 1 admin

**Prize pool example (canonical — use this in How It Works contexts):**
> 5 players at 5,000 naira each. Total pool: 25,000 naira. Platform takes 10%.
> Prize pool: 22,500 naira. Winner Takes All: top scorer takes 22,500 naira.
> Top 3 Split: 1st takes 10,125 naira, 2nd takes 6,750 naira, 3rd takes 5,625 naira.

## 6. Audience Profiles

### Challengers (Arena)
- Nigerian adults and young professionals who studied hard and want something to show for it
- University students and graduates across any subject
- Students in active exam preparation who want to earn while they study
- Under-18 users who want to build their rank now and access staking at 18
- International users who join for practice and tier progression (staking is Nigeria-only at launch)

**What they care about:** Real stakes. A rank that means something. Fairness.
**What they fear:** Being scammed. Losing to someone who cheated. Wasting their time.
**Copy approach:** Lead with what they earn or prove. Be specific about numbers and rules.
Never over-promise on the money side.

### Gift Card Beta Testers (100 spots only)
- The most valuable early user type for regulatory and mechanics validation
- Gets N10,000 in gift cards (two N5,000 cards) to test the real-money loop
- Wallet withdrawals not active during beta — frame this as a platform limitation, not a
  restriction on the user

**Copy approach:** Make the scarcity real (100 spots). Make the mechanics concrete.
Never say "cannot withdraw" — say "withdrawals are not active during beta."

### Standalone Instructors
- Teachers, tutors, subject-matter experts who want passive income from their knowledge
- Do not compete. Write questions. Earn monthly based on usage in staking sessions.

**Copy approach:** Lead with the earn mechanic. Specificity on the payout formula builds
trust. The 1% withdrawal commission (vs 3% for Challengers) is a meaningful distinction —
use it.

### Sponsors
- Nigerian brands and organizations (funded competitions — 15% commission)
- International brands (free competitions — no prize pool, no commission)
- Marketing managers who need measurable engagement, not just impressions

**Copy approach:** Lead with control and data. "Your brand inside a live competition, not
a banner beside one." For funded sponsors: cost clarity is the pitch (one-time cost, no
subscription). For free sponsors: the data is the pitch.

### Schools and Tutorial Centers (EDU)
- Secondary school admins and principals
- Tutorial center owners
- Target: mid-size schools (50+ students) who hit the free tier ceiling immediately

**Copy approach:** Speak to the admin, not the student. Cost, control, data ownership.
Never say "subscription" on EDU pages — schools distrust recurring fees. Use "term billing"
or "pay per account per term." The FailFast name should be introduced alongside the
philosophy, never cold — lead with "fail fast and learn faster" before naming it.

## 7. Audience-Specific Framing Rules

### Nigeria-first, globally inclusive
The platform's story is Nigerian. The founding moment (secondary school students staking
on past question scores) is the credibility. Do not genericize this story.

**What stays Nigerian:**
- The problem section opening ("You scored 290 in JAMB")
- "Real naira" as the specific earning denomination
- "Nigerian Challengers" when describing who funded competitions reach

**What should be universally readable:**
- Bullet examples of who uses the platform (no exam-specific names like WAEC or JAMB
  in bullets — use "students in active exam preparation" instead)
- Access language on Challengers card ("Open to users worldwide")
- FAQ answers about international and under-18 access

### Under-18 users
- Can register, practice, build tiers, unlock features, compete in free challenges
- Cannot access wallet, staking, or funded competitions
- Everything carries over when they turn 18 — this is a selling point, not a footnote
- Do not block or discourage them — frame it as building their head start

### International users
- Global access is organic — do not actively market to them
- The FAQ handles their questions. The Challengers card access line handles their eligibility.
- Do not add dedicated international user bullets or banners — this contradicts the
  organic growth strategy

## 8. What to Never Write

### Banned AI vocabulary
These words are forbidden in FailFast copy:
`delve`, `tapestry`, `vibrant`, `pivotal`, `crucial`, `meticulous`, `underscore` (as verb),
`showcase`, `bolster`, `garner`, `foster`, `cultivate`, `enhance`, `encompass`, `intricate`,
`interplay`, `landscape` (abstract), `testament`, `enduring`, `stands as`, `serves as`,
`marks a`, `represents a`, `valuable insights`, `align with`, `resonate with`,
`Additionally` (to start a sentence), `not just`, `not only`, `setting the stage`,
`groundbreaking`, `renowned`, `diverse array`, `commitment to`, `in the heart of`,
`seamlessly`, `effortlessly`, `transformative`, `leverage`, `utilize`, `synergy`,
`holistic`, `innovative`, `world-class`, `cutting-edge`, `robust`, `ecosystem`,
`unlock potential`, `journey` (metaphorical)

### Banned structural patterns
- Em dashes in live copy — use colons, commas, or a full stop instead
- Sentences that only say "this is important" — show importance through specifics
- Section summaries that restate what was just said
- Hollow -ing closers: "...creating a better experience for users."
- "Not just X, but also Y" constructions
- Any phrasing that frames a platform limitation as a user restriction
  (wrong: "cannot withdraw" — right: "withdrawals are not active during beta")

### Banned terms in specific contexts
- "Subscription" on EDU pages
- "Verification pipeline" in live copy (internal term — use "review process")
- "AI check" or "AI-assisted review" in live copy (sounds like cheating detection —
  use "automated screening")
- "Ad interstitials" anywhere user-facing — use plain language
- "Brand impression count" — use "how many times your brand was displayed"
- "Anonymised" in Sponsors copy — just remove the clause

## 9. Page-by-Page Standards

### Homepage
- Hero headline is fixed: `Fail fast. Correct faster. Get paid.` — do not soften or reorder
- Problem section opens with Nigerian exam culture — do not genericize
- How It Works uses three steps with outcome lines in green text
- Scoring table is the primary evidence of the fail-forward claim — never summarize it away
- Beta notice must acknowledge the licensing situation plainly and without spin
- Mid-page CTA closes on what early members carry into the live platform, not generic
  "first to know" language

### For Schools (EDU)
- Speak to the admin, not the student
- Hero framing: control, privacy, data, no Arena bleed-through
- Pricing section: term-based billing, account-day tracking, no surprises
- CTA: "We are looking for five schools to build this with." — partnership language, not
  product signup language
- Never use "subscription"

### For Instructors
- Lead with the earn mechanic in the hero
- Payout formula explained concretely with worked example
- 1% withdrawal commission vs 3% for Challengers — mention it, it earns trust
- "Your questions go into live staking sessions" — active ownership language

### For Sponsors
- Free and funded competitions are both options — hero subhead should not imply funded-only
- "Set up a branded competition" — not "fund a competition"
- Cost is one-time, not subscription — say it explicitly
- Sponsor tiers are based on total payouts from funded competitions only

## 10. App UI and Product Copy Patterns

When writing strings for the actual application (not the waitlist site):

**Button labels:** Verb-first, specific. "Join Challenge" not "Participate". "Submit Questions" not "Upload". "Withdraw Funds" not "Cash Out".

**Empty states:** Tell the user what to do, not what is missing. "Start a FailFast Mine session to earn your first Grit Points." not "No points yet."

**Error messages:** Non-accusatory. State what happened, then what to do. "This challenge is full. Browse other open challenges." not "You cannot join this challenge."

**Onboarding copy:** One concept per screen. Use "you" throughout. Avoid explaining the scoring system in prose — the table does it better. Let users see the table before they play.

**Notifications:** One sentence. Outcome first. "Your question passed verification and is now in the pool." not "Congratulations! We are pleased to inform you that your submitted question has successfully passed our review process."

**Wallet and payment copy:** Fee-forward. Always show the exact amount before confirming. "You will receive 9,700 naira. The 3% withdrawal fee of 300 naira is deducted before transfer." Users must see the full breakdown before confirming any transaction.

**Tier promotion screens:** Celebrate the arrival, not the distance remaining. Lead with what just unlocked. Show "progress to next tier" as secondary, not primary.

## 11. Marketing Content Patterns

### Email subject lines
- Specific over clever. "Your FailFast rank is building. Join before the waitlist closes." beats "The future of learning is here."
- Avoid exclamation marks
- Never start with "We are excited to..."

### Push notifications
- One action or one outcome. Never both in the same notification.
- "A new sponsored competition just opened in Biology. 5,000 naira prize pool." — good.
- "Check out the exciting new competitions on FailFast today!" — never.

### Social media (X/Twitter @failFastNG)
- Platform voice carries through — direct, specific, numbers-forward
- Nigerian context is the default — the audience is Nigerian
- Avoid reposting generic EdTech content — FailFast is a specific product with a specific mechanic
- Thread format works well for explaining the scoring system or tier structure

### Launch announcement copy
The platform launches in free challenges mode. Real-money staking requires the gaming
license. Any launch announcement must be honest about this. The correct framing:
"Real challenges. Real ranks. Real money comes when the license is confirmed."
Never promise staking in marketing before the license is granted.

## 12. Quick Self-Check Before Submitting Any FailFast Copy

1. Did I use the correct product name casing? (`failFastNG`, `FailFast Arena`, `FailFast EDU`, `FailFast Mine`)
2. Did I use Grit Points and Success Points correctly?
3. Did I say "group challenge" instead of "competition" or "game"?
4. Did I say "real naira" instead of "real money"?
5. Did I use "term" instead of "quarter" on EDU pages?
6. Is the commission rate correct for the context? (10% staking, 15% funded sponsor, 5% hosted)
7. Did I frame any platform limitation as a user restriction? (fix it if yes)
8. Did I use any banned AI vocabulary?
9. Did I use an em dash in live copy? (use colon, comma, or full stop instead)
10. Does the copy end on what the user gets, not what the platform does?
11. Is there a specific number I could use instead of a vague phrase?
12. Would a Nigerian who just finished exam season read this and trust it?

If the answer to any of 7–11 is yes, revise before submitting.
